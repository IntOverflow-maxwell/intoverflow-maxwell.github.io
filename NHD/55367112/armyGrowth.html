ROME'S ARMY
By the late 2nd century B.C, The Roman republic was a powerhouse in the Mediterranean, however the republic is facing challenges from all sides as seen before in defeats in Cimbri and Jugurtha. The depleted manpower could not sustain the need of the army and the soldiers were unmotivated to participate as it left their property untaken care of. Within the army soldiers were separated on the armors they can afford, which resulted in the separation of classes, and unequal fighting capabilities. This often resulted in low morale and were often overrun by the charges of Barbaric tribes.
Vercingetorix Throwing down His Weapons at the feet of Julius Caesar, Lionel Royer
The Marius reforms allowed men without property to join the army with equipment purchased by the state, addressing the manpower shortage in Rome, allowing for bigger army to be raised to deal with the foes on various fronts, and changed soldiers from duty to a profession, which motivated soldiers more. The organization of the army was transformed with soldiers now organized into cohorts, no longer separated and organized into uniform heavy infantry; instilling discipline and standard to the soldiers, raising morale and making the army more resistant to charges of barbaric tribes, closing in the gaps between different groups.
A Roman legion, Marco Dente
In addition, Marius had redesigned the logistics by letting the soldiers carrying their own equipment, greatly increasing the maneuverability of the Roman army, which is essential for the expansion of Roman republic. In Caesar’s gallic campaigns, he once marched 6 legions by 15 kilometers in 3 hours, compared to the 9 day march of 230 km during the Jugurthine war. Such changes also gave the soldiers an opportunity to train their stamina and the simplified logistics gave the army an opportunity to maneuver.

The reforms had shifted the Roman military landscape and laid the foundation for Rome to turn into one of the greatest empires in mankind's history. The landless people that were recruited had no incentive to leave the army, eventually leading to the change from seasonal army during campaign season, to permanent army that is stationary at every part of Rome. The reorganizing of the army into heavy infantry however also meant a lack of cavalries. To address to this problem, Rome tended to rely on the cavalries of its allies, such as Caesar relying on Gallic horseman during his campaign in Gaul. This reliance on cavalries of its allies had allowed for Rome expand greatly, but also welcomed and integrated those that had been conquered. This attitude to integrate and allowing all conquered people to serve Rome had led to the rise of Rome but also their downfall. By the late Roman empire, too many Germanic people had been entering the empire, destabilizing their society, forming factions among the military, which is also a consequence of the Marian reforms.
